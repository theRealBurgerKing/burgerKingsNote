## Question 1: Robust Regression

**Background.** We study linear regression under Huber's contamination model:

$$P_\varepsilon = (1-\varepsilon)P_0 + \varepsilon Q$$

where $Q$ is an outlier distribution and $\varepsilon \in [0,1]$ is the contamination fraction. Two contamination mechanisms are considered:

- **(I) Response contamination**: covariates unchanged; Gaussian noise replaced by Cauchy noise $\tilde{\eta}_i \sim \text{Cauchy}(0, \sigma)$.
- **(II) Covariate contamination**: covariate distribution inflated by factor $\tau > 1$; Gaussian noise unchanged.

## (b) Gradient Contribution under MSE Loss

Recall the MSE loss:

$$L(\beta) = \frac{1}{2n}\sum_{i=1}^n L_i(\beta)^2, \qquad L_i(\beta) = y_i - x_i^\top\beta.$$

**Gradient derivation:**

$$\frac{\partial}{\partial \beta} \frac{1}{2n} L_i(\beta)^2 = \frac{1}{n} L_i(\beta) \cdot \frac{\partial L_i(\beta)}{\partial \beta} = \frac{1}{n} L_i(\beta) \cdot (-x_i)$$

Summing over all observations:

$$\nabla_\beta L(\beta) = -\frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n L_i(\beta)\,x_i$$

The gradient contribution of observation $i$ is $-\frac{1}{n}L_i(\beta)\,x_i$, which is **proportional to $L_i(\beta) \cdot x_i$**.

**Why both contamination types cause instability:**

- **Response contamination**: Cauchy noise generates arbitrarily large residuals $|L_i(\beta)|$. A single outlier can produce a massive gradient update, destabilising convergence.
- **Covariate contamination**: Even with moderate residuals, a contaminated point with $\|x_i\|_2 \gg 1$ (inflated by factor $\tau$) amplifies the gradient contribution through the $x_i$ factor, causing large and erratic updates.

## (c) Huber ($L_\delta$) Loss Analysis

Define:

$$\ell_\delta(r) = \begin{cases} \frac{1}{2}r^2, & |r| \le \delta \\ \delta\!\left(|r| - \frac{1}{2}\delta\right), & |r| > \delta \end{cases}, \qquad L_\delta(\beta) = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n \ell_\delta\!\left(y_i - x_i^\top\beta\right).$$

**1. Is $L_\delta$ better suited to deal with response contamination?**

Yes. For large residuals ($|r| > \delta$), $\ell_\delta$ grows **linearly** rather than quadratically. The derivative is capped at $\pm\delta$, so even Cauchy outliers with enormous residuals produce bounded gradient contributions. This directly mitigates the instability identified in (b).

**2. What is the role of $\delta$?**

$\delta$ is the threshold separating the quadratic regime (efficient on clean data, behaves like MSE) from the linear regime (robust to outliers). Smaller $\delta$ increases robustness but reduces statistical efficiency. It acts as a **robustness–efficiency trade-off parameter**.

**3. What is the relationship between $L_\delta$ and MSE?**

As $\delta \to \infty$, the condition $|r| > \delta$ is never triggered, so $\ell_\delta(r) \to \frac{1}{2}r^2$ for all $r$, and $L_\delta \to$ MSE. Huber loss is a **smooth interpolation** between MSE (quadratic, non-robust) and MAE (linear, robust).

**4. Is $L_\delta$ better suited to deal with covariate contamination?**

Not directly. Huber loss caps the residual factor $\psi_\delta(r_i)$, but the gradient is still $-\frac{1}{n}\psi_\delta(r_i)\,x_i$. If $\|x_i\|_2$ is very large due to covariate contamination, the gradient update can still be large. Addressing covariate contamination requires additional techniques such as covariate clipping (see part (g)).

## (d) Gradient Descent Update Rules

Let $X \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times d}$, $y \in \mathbb{R}^n$, $\gamma > 0$.

**MSE loss:**

$$L(\beta) = \frac{1}{2n}\|y - X\beta\|^2 \implies \nabla_\beta L(\beta) = -\frac{1}{n}X^\top(y - X\beta)$$

$$\boxed{\beta^{(t+1)} = \beta^{(t)} + \frac{\gamma}{n}X^\top\!\left(y - X\beta^{(t)}\right)}$$

**$L_\delta$ loss:**

Using $\frac{d}{dr}\ell_\delta(r) = \psi_\delta(r)$ where

$$\psi_\delta(r) = \begin{cases}r, & |r|\le\delta\\ \delta\,\text{sign}(r), & |r|>\delta\end{cases}$$

and chain rule with $r_i = y_i - x_i^\top\beta$, $\frac{\partial r_i}{\partial\beta} = -x_i$:

$$\nabla_\beta L_\delta(\beta) = -\frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n \psi_\delta(r_i)\,x_i = -\frac{1}{n}X^\top\psi_\delta(y - X\beta)$$

where $\psi_\delta(\cdot)$ is applied elementwise. Therefore:

$$\boxed{\beta^{(t+1)} = \beta^{(t)} + \frac{\gamma}{n}X^\top\psi_\delta\!\left(y - X\beta^{(t)}\right)}$$

## (e) Gradient Descent Experiments

**Setup**: $d = 20$, $n = 100$, $\beta^* = \mathbf{1}_d$, $\gamma = 0.05$, $\delta = 1.345$, $T = 50$, 10 independent trials. Response contamination with $\varepsilon \in \{0, 0.1, 0.4\}$ (rows), $\sigma \in \{0, 1, 5\}$ (columns).

> **[INSERT FIGURE: `qe_gradient_descent.png`]**

**Discussion:**

1. **Is MSE generally monotonically decreasing?** On clean data ($\varepsilon = 0$, $\sigma = 0$), yes — GD converges smoothly. With contamination, MSE is **not monotonically decreasing**: large Cauchy residuals produce erratic updates that can temporarily increase $\|\beta^{(t)} - \beta^*\|_2$. The Huber loss converges more reliably because $\psi_\delta$ caps gradient magnitudes.

2. **Effect of increasing $\varepsilon$ (fixed $\sigma$)?** More contaminated points means more large-gradient events. MSE trajectories become more variable and the average trajectory converges to a worse final value. Huber is comparatively stable.

3. **Effect of increasing $\sigma$ (fixed $\varepsilon$)?** Heavier Cauchy tails produce even larger residuals per outlier. MSE individual runs become increasingly erratic and may diverge. Huber individual runs remain stable, confirming that **$\delta$-loss is strongly preferred when significant noise contamination is suspected**.

## (f) Stochastic Gradient Descent Experiments

**Setup**: same as (e) but replace GD with SGD — at each iteration, select $i_t$ uniformly at random from $\{1,\ldots,n\}$ and update using only that observation.

> **[INSERT FIGURE: `qf_sgd.png`]**

**Is SGD more sensitive than GD to response contamination?**

**Yes, SGD is more sensitive.** Mathematical reasoning:

- In GD, the gradient averages over all $n$ samples: $-\frac{1}{n}\sum_i L_i(\beta)x_i$. The outlier effect is **diluted** by averaging.
- In SGD, the update at step $t$ uses only $i_t$: gradient $= -L_{i_t}(\beta)\,x_{i_t}$. If $i_t$ is an outlier with a large Cauchy residual, the **entire update is controlled by that single corrupted point** with no averaging to dampen it.
- Under Huber loss, $\psi_\delta$ caps $|L_{i_t}|$ at $\delta$, so even if an outlier is selected, the step magnitude is bounded. This explains why SGD Huber runs remain relatively stable while SGD MSE runs can diverge at large $\sigma$.
- The individual MSE-SGD trajectories show extreme variance for high $\varepsilon$ and $\sigma$, confirming this analysis.

## (g) Covariate Clipping

Replace each covariate $x_i$ with the clipped version:

$$\tilde{x}_i = x_i \cdot \min\!\left(1,\, \frac{R}{\|x_i\|_2}\right)$$

Let $\tilde{X}$ denote the matrix with rows $\tilde{x}_i^\top$.

**1. Gradient descent update with $L_\delta$ loss and clipped design matrix $\tilde{X}$:**

The update rule from (d) applies directly with $\tilde{X}$ replacing $X$:

$$\boxed{\beta^{(t+1)} = \beta^{(t)} + \frac{\gamma}{n}\tilde{X}^\top\psi_\delta\!\left(y - \tilde{X}\beta^{(t)}\right)}$$

**2. Why clipping is useful for each contamination type:**

- **Response contamination**: Limited benefit. The outlier is in $y_i$, not $x_i$. Since clean and contaminated observations share the same covariate distribution, clipping does not distinguish them. The $L_\delta$ loss itself is the appropriate remedy.
- **Covariate contamination**: Directly addresses the problem. Contaminated points have $\|x_i\|_2 \approx \tau\sqrt{d} \gg R$ for a reasonable $R$, so they get clipped to norm $R$. This **bounds the gradient contribution** of every observation to at most $\frac{\gamma R}{n}\cdot\|\psi_\delta(\cdot)\|_\infty$ per step, eliminating the destabilising effect of inflated covariates.

## (h) Soft Rule vs. Hard Rule

**Setup**: clean distribution $P_0$: $x \sim \mathcal{N}(0, I_d)$, $y = x^\top\beta^* + \eta$, $\eta \sim \mathcal{N}(0,1)$. Contamination $Q$: $x = ae_1$ ($a \gg 1$), $y = 0$.

**When $\varepsilon$ is small (few outliers):**

The **Hard Rule is more reliable**. With few outliers, the first-step OLS estimate is close to $\beta^*$. The contaminated points (with $y = 0$ but fitted value $\approx a\beta^*_1$) will have anomalously large residuals and are correctly identified and removed. The refit on the remaining clean data is nearly unbiased. The Soft Rule also works, but with very few outliers the two procedures perform similarly.

**When $\varepsilon$ is moderately large (many outliers):**

The **Soft Rule ($L_\delta$) is more reliable**. With many outliers, the first-step OLS used by the Hard Rule is itself heavily contaminated — it is pulled toward the outlier cluster. As a result, residuals on true outliers may not be the largest (OLS has partially fit them), while some clean points may appear to have large residuals. This **masking effect** causes the Hard Rule to misidentify outliers: it may remove clean points while retaining corrupted ones. The Soft Rule avoids this entirely by robustly down-weighting high-residual observations throughout optimisation, without relying on a clean first-step estimate.
