## Question 1(b) — MSE Gradient and Instability

The MSE loss is:
$$L(\beta) = \frac{1}{2n}\sum_{i=1}^n (y_i - x_i^\top\beta)^2$$

Taking the gradient with respect to $\beta$:
$$\nabla_\beta L(\beta) = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n (y_i - x_i^\top\beta)\cdot\nabla_\beta(y_i - x_i^\top\beta) = -\frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n (y_i - x_i^\top\beta)\,x_i$$

The gradient contribution of observation $i$ is therefore:
$$g_i(\beta) = -L_i(\beta)\,x_i = -(y_i - x_i^\top\beta)\,x_i$$

which is proportional to $L_i(\beta)\,x_i$.

**Why both contamination mechanisms cause instability:**

- **Response contamination:** Cauchy noise has no finite variance, so $|y_i - x_i^\top\beta|$ can be arbitrarily large. A single outlier produces an unbounded gradient contribution, dominating the entire update and pushing $\beta$ far from $\beta^*$.

- **Covariate contamination:** Contaminated points have $\|x_i\|_2 \approx \tau\sqrt{d}$, inflated by factor $\tau$. Even with a normal residual, the large $x_i$ directly amplifies the gradient contribution by factor $\tau$. If both the residual and covariate are large, the effect is compounded.

In both cases, MSE applies no bound on gradient contributions, making it highly sensitive to outliers.

## Question 1(c) — Huber Loss Analysis

The Huber loss is defined as:
$$\ell_\delta(r) = \begin{cases} \frac{1}{2}r^2 & |r| \leq \delta \\ \delta(|r| - \frac{1}{2}\delta) & |r| > \delta \end{cases}$$

**Derivative:** For $|r| \leq \delta$: $\frac{d}{dr}\ell_\delta(r) = r$. For $|r| > \delta$, using $\frac{d}{dr}|r| = \text{sign}(r)$:
$$\frac{d}{dr}\ell_\delta(r) = \delta\cdot\text{sign}(r)$$

The gradient contribution of observation $i$ under $L_\delta$ is $-\psi_\delta(r_i)\,x_i$, where:
$$\psi_\delta(r) = \begin{cases} r & |r| \leq \delta \\ \delta\,\text{sign}(r) & |r| > \delta \end{cases}$$

**1. Is $L_\delta$ better suited for response contamination?**

Yes. For large residuals ($|r|>\delta$), the gradient contribution is capped at $\delta\cdot\|x_i\|_2$, regardless of how extreme the residual is. This prevents Cauchy-distributed outliers from dominating the gradient update.

**2. What is the role of $\delta$?**

$\delta$ is the threshold between quadratic and linear behaviour. For $|r|\leq\delta$, $L_\delta$ behaves identically to MSE. For $|r|>\delta$, it switches to linear loss with constant gradient magnitude $\delta$. Smaller $\delta$ gives more robustness but reduces efficiency on clean data; larger $\delta$ approaches MSE behaviour.

**3. Relationship between $L_\delta$ and MSE:**

As $\delta\to\infty$, all residuals satisfy $|r|\leq\delta$, and $L_\delta(\beta)\to\frac{1}{2n}\sum r_i^2 = L_\text{MSE}(\beta)$. MSE is the limiting case of Huber loss, and $L_\delta$ interpolates between MSE ($\delta\to\infty$) and MAE ($\delta\to 0$).

**4. Is $L_\delta$ better suited for covariate contamination?**

Only partially. Huber loss truncates the residual component of the gradient, bounding it at $\delta$. However, it does not address the inflated covariate norm $\|x_i\|_2$. The gradient contribution is still $\propto \delta\cdot\|x_i\|_2$, so large covariates continue to have outsized influence. Huber loss alone is insufficient for covariate contamination.

## Question 1(d) — Gradient Descent Updates

**MSE loss.** From (b): $\nabla_\beta L(\beta) = -\frac{1}{n}X^\top(y - X\beta)$. Applying the GD rule $\beta^{(t+1)} = \beta^{(t)} - \gamma\nabla L(\beta^{(t)})$:

$$\boxed{\beta^{(t+1)} = \beta^{(t)} + \frac{\gamma}{n}X^\top\!\left(y - X\beta^{(t)}\right)}$$

**Huber loss.** With $r_i = y_i - x_i^\top\beta$:
$$\frac{\partial}{\partial\beta}\ell_\delta(r_i) = \frac{\partial\ell_\delta}{\partial r_i}\cdot\frac{\partial r_i}{\partial\beta} = \psi_\delta(r_i)\cdot(-x_i)$$

Summing over all observations:
$$\nabla_\beta L_\delta(\beta) = -\frac{1}{n}X^\top\psi_\delta(y - X\beta)$$

Applying the GD rule:

$$\boxed{\beta^{(t+1)} = \beta^{(t)} + \frac{\gamma}{n}X^\top\psi_\delta\!\left(y - X\beta^{(t)}\right)}$$

where $\psi_\delta(\cdot)$ is applied elementwise. The two updates are structurally identical; the only difference is that Huber clips the residual vector through $\psi_\delta$ before multiplying by $X^\top$.

## Question 1(f) — SGD Experiment

*See attached plots and code.py.*

**Does SGD amplify sensitivity to response contamination compared to GD?**

Yes. SGD is more sensitive to response contamination than GD for the following reason.

GD computes the full gradient average over all $n$ samples each step:
$$\nabla L(\beta) = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n g_i(\beta)$$
The $\varepsilon n$ contaminated points contribute, but their extreme gradients are diluted by averaging with the remaining $(1-\varepsilon)n$ clean points. A single outlier contributes only $1/n$ of the total gradient.

SGD selects one sample per step uniformly at random. With probability $\varepsilon$, the selected sample is a contaminated point, and its gradient **entirely determines** the update for that step. At $\varepsilon=0.4$, roughly 2 in every 5 steps are controlled by an outlier, repeatedly pushing $\beta$ away from $\beta^*$ with no compensating signal from clean data.

This is confirmed in the plots: SGD individual runs show much greater spread than their GD counterparts, especially in the high-contamination regime ($\varepsilon=0.4$, $\sigma=5$). MSE+SGD individual runs frequently diverge, while Huber+SGD runs remain more concentrated, though still more variable than Huber+GD.

## Question 1(g) — Covariate Clipping

**1. GD update with clipped design matrix:**

Define $\tilde{x}_i = x_i\cdot\min\!\left(1,\, \frac{R}{\|x_i\|_2}\right)$, so $\|\tilde{x}_i\|_2 \leq R$ for all $i$. Let $\tilde{X}$ be the matrix with rows $\tilde{x}_i^\top$. Substituting into the Huber GD update from (d):

$$\boxed{\beta^{(t+1)} = \beta^{(t)} + \frac{\gamma}{n}\tilde{X}^\top\psi_\delta\!\left(y - \tilde{X}\beta^{(t)}\right)}$$

**2. Why clipping is useful:**

- **Covariate contamination (direct):** Contaminated points have $\|x_i\|_2\approx\tau\sqrt{d}$, which can be very large. Clipping projects all covariates onto the ball of radius $R$, so no single point has a disproportionately large $\|x_i\|_2$. Combined with Huber's residual truncation, the gradient contribution of any single observation is bounded above by $\delta R$, eliminating the leverage effect of contaminated covariates.

- **Response contamination (indirect):** Clean covariate norms are approximately $\sqrt{d}$. Setting $R \approx \sqrt{d}$ leaves clean points nearly unchanged while providing a safety margin against any covariate that happens to have an unusually large norm, further stabilising the updates.

Together, Huber loss (caps residual) and covariate clipping (caps covariate norm) provide dual protection against both contamination mechanisms.

## Question 1(h) — Soft Rule vs Hard Rule

**Setup:** Contaminated observations are fixed at $x = ae_1$ ($a\gg 1$), $y=0$, concentrated at a large value of the first coordinate.

**When $\varepsilon$ is small:**

Both procedures perform reasonably, but the soft rule (Huber loss on all data) is more reliable. The hard rule relies on detecting outliers via OLS residuals. However, high-leverage points such as $x=ae_1$ can pull the OLS fit toward them, resulting in small residuals for the very outliers we wish to detect. This **masking effect** means contaminated points may not be flagged for removal. The soft rule avoids this issue entirely by downweighting large residuals automatically.

**When $\varepsilon$ is moderately large:**

The soft rule is substantially more reliable. With many contaminated points, the initial OLS fit in the hard rule is heavily biased — the fitted line is pulled far from the true $\beta^*$. Residual-based detection then operates from a corrupted baseline: clean points may appear as outliers and be incorrectly removed, while contaminated points appear well-fitted and survive. This **swamping effect** further degrades performance. The soft rule does not require a preliminary fit; it simultaneously estimates $\beta$ and limits the influence of outliers, maintaining robustness as long as $\varepsilon < 0.5$.

**Conclusion:** The soft rule is preferred in both regimes. The hard rule is fragile because its first-stage OLS estimate is itself vulnerable to the contamination it is trying to correct.
